
# sandbags

In 32mm/heroic scale, commercial sandbags are often sculpted slightly oversized for visual readability.

So you have two options:

## A — True-scale (historically accurate)

* 10 mm long
* 5 mm wide
* 4 mm high

## B — Hobby-friendly (slightly heroic)

* 12–14 mm long
* 6–7 mm wide
* 4–5 mm high


# sandbags painting

Make believable sandbags: dusty, desaturated, slightly dirty canvas with soft transitions — oils excel at this.

Your black primer will help create depth if you keep the oils thin.

## Recommended oil colors

* Yellow Ochre
* Raw Umber
* Burnt Umber
* Titanium White
* Payne’s Grey (optional, for cool shadows)
* Burnt Sienna (optional, for warmer tones)

## Tools

* Odorless thinner
* Old synthetic brushes (oils are gentle, but don’t ruin your best)
* Cotton buds / makeup sponges
* Small piece of cardboard (to leech excess oil)

## Overview of the process

Lean oil base → wipe → blend → add texture → add dust.
The whole method is about exploiting oils’ long open time.

## Step by step

### 1) Reduce the shine: apply a thin black-to-dark brown underlayer (optional but recommended)

Because you primed in black, mix:

Raw Umber + a touch of Yellow Ochre

Thin it heavily with OMS → apply as a wash, letting the black stay in the deepest folds.

Let this settle for 10–15 min.

This gives you a base warmer than raw black.

### 2) Apply the main sandbag color (lean oil glaze)

On cardboard for 10 minutes place:

* Yellow Ochre
* Raw Umber
* Titanium White

This removes excess linseed oil → faster dry.

Then make a glaze:
60% Yellow Ochre + 30% Raw Umber + 10% White

Add a few drops of thinner to make it fluid (not thick).

Brush it on thinly — let the black primer underneath show through slightly.

### 3) Wipe to reveal texture

Using a dry cotton bud, gently wipe the ridges and top-facing surfaces.

This does two things:

* Reveals highlights naturally
* Keeps shadows dark

Instantly gives a dusty "fabric" feel

If you like more texture:
Use a makeup sponge very lightly to dab → gives canvas-like grain.

### 4) Add shadow depth

While still wet, push shadows into folds using:

Raw Umber + Payne’s Grey (90/10)

Use a very fine brush.
Blend with a clean soft brush (oils make perfect soft transitions).

### 5) Add warm highlights

For realistic canvas:

Add Titanium White + Yellow Ochre (80/20)
Place on top edges and top surfaces.
Blend outward gently.

Canvas should NEVER be pure white — always slightly warm.

### 6) Add variation (key realism step)

Sandbags are never uniform. Add tiny tint variations:

* Add tiny touches of Burnt Sienna → rusty / dirty
* Add tiny touches of Payne’s Grey → damp / shadowed
* Add tiny touches of Raw Umber → aged canvas

Blend all in softly. Oils allow micro-variation beautifully.

### 7) Dry-brush (optional, but only after dry)

In 24–48 hours, once the oils are matte, you can dry-brush lightly with acrylic:

* Buff → for dusty look
* Khaki → for sharp grain highlights

Not required, but adds “scale realism.”

### 8) Dusting (optional but great for WW1 terrain)

After fully dry (2–4 days), add powdered pigments:

* Light earth
* Dust
* Soot around creases

Fix with very light pigment fixer or matte varnish.

## Quick reference recipe

* Sandbag Base (oil): Yellow Ochre + Raw Umber
* Shadows: Raw Umber + tiny Payne’s Grey
* Highlights: Yellow Ochre + White (warm)
* Variation: Burnt Sienna dots + blend
* Finish: Dust tones (pigments)

Here are the most reliable formulas:

## 1) The classic mud mix (most natural)

* Raw Umber + Yellow Ochre + a touch of Ultramarine Blue
* Raw Umber → earth base
* Yellow Ochre → warm soil undertone
* Ultramarine Blue → adds natural coolness and neutralizes the yellow

This gives a very “ground-realistic” mud, great for terrain, trenches, sandbags
stains, etc.

## 2) Cold, wet mud

* Burnt Umber + Ultramarine Blue + a tiny bit of Black
* Burnt Umber → reddish brown
* Ultramarine → cools it
* Black → adds moisture/darkness

Good for soaked trench mud, wheel ruts, boot prints.

## 3) Light brown mud (dry muddy patches)

* Yellow Ochre + Burnt Sienna + White + a touch of Raw Umber

This gives the dry top layer / chalky mud seen in late summer or dried
riverbeds.

## 4) Mud from only primaries

If you only have Red + Yellow + Blue:

* Mix equal parts Yellow + Red → orange
* Add Blue until it becomes brown
* Add a tiny bit more Blue or a speck of Black to desaturate
* Darken or lighten with Black/White

This gives a surprisingly convincing earth tone.

## Adjusting your mud

* Too warm/orange? → add Blue or Raw Umber
* Too cold/grey? → add Yellow Ochre
* Too green? → add Burnt Sienna
* Too dark? → mix in Yellow Ochre + White
* Too light? → add Burnt Umber or Ultramarine Blue

## For your terrain & wargaming use

Since you often paint WW1 trench terrain, PLA/XPS, sandbags, wooden planks,
etc.:

* Base: Raw Umber
* Shade: Raw Umber + Ultramarine
* Highlight: Raw Umber + Yellow Ochre + a bit of White
* Wet patches: Burnt Umber + a touch of Black
* Gloss varnish over the darkest spots → perfect “wet mud”
